<span class="sentence">      Although many accounts of task switching emphasize the importance of working memory as a substantial source of the switch cost, there is a lack of evidence demonstrating that task switching actually places additional demands on working memory. </span><span class="sentence">The present study addressed this issue by implementing task switching in continuous complex span tasks with strictly controlled time parameters. </span><span class="sentence">A series of 4 experiments demonstrate that recall performance decreased as a function of the number of task switches and that the concurrent load of item maintenance had no influence on task switching. </span><span class="sentence">These results indicate that task switching induces a cost on working memory functioning. </span><span class="sentence">Implications for theories of task switching, working memory, and resource sharing are addressed.</span>
