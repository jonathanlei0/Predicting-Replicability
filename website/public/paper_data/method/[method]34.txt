<h3>Experiment 1</h3><br/><p class="body-paragraph" data-auto="body_paragraph"><span class="sentence">In Experiment 1, we compared the magnitude of the sum effect for cue digits (e.g., 3 + 4) that were primed by aligned categorically related word pairs (e.g., <em>ants</em>–<em>beetles</em>) and by misaligned unrelated word pairs (e.g., <em>hens</em>–<em>radios</em>). </span><span class="sentence">According to Bassok et al. (1998), unlike categorically related sets that preserve a one-to-one mapping with the structure of addition (e.g., 4 ants + 3 beetles = 7 insects), semantically unrelated sets are misaligned with addition. </span><span class="sentence">This misalignment arises because one would need to traverse many links in the semantic network to find the superset that corresponds to their sum (e.g., 4 hens + 3 radios = 7 things). </span><span class="sentence">Such intermediate links (e.g., hens are farm birds, which are animate creatures, which are things) introduce relational mismatches with the operation of addition, in which the addends are directly related to their sum.</span></p> <h4>Method</h4><p class="body-paragraph" data-auto="body_paragraph"><strong>Participants</strong></p><p class="body-paragraph" data-auto="body_paragraph"><span class="sentence">One hundred fifty-four University of Washington introductory psychology students, 91 women and 63 men, participated for course extra credit. </span><span class="sentence">All were native English speakers with normal or corrected vision.</span></p><p class="body-paragraph" data-auto="body_paragraph"><strong>Materials</strong></p><p class="body-paragraph" data-auto="body_paragraph"><span class="sentence">The trial stimuli were random combinations of 80 word triplets and 48 digit triplets. </span><span class="sentence">Each trial consisted of a priming word pair (e.g., <em>tulips–daisies</em>) with its word target (e.g., <em>tulips</em>) and a pair of cue digits (e.g., <em>2 + 3</em>) with its digit target (e.g., <em>5</em>). </span><span class="sentence">Below, we first describe the word and the digit triplets and then explain how the word and the digit triplets were combined.</span></p><p class="body-paragraph" data-auto="body_paragraph"><em>Word triplets</em></p><p class="body-paragraph" data-auto="body_paragraph"><span class="sentence">All word stimuli were plural nouns that could serve as referents of object sets (e.g., <em>birds</em>, <em>coats</em>, <em>thieves</em>, <em>stories</em>). </span><span class="sentence">We first selected a set of candidate singular nouns from word-norms databases (Battig &amp; Montague, 1969; Coltheart, 1981). </span><span class="sentence">The candidate words were in the upper 10% of word frequency, familiarity, and concreteness. </span><span class="sentence">Their syllable count was restricted to be between one and five. </span><span class="sentence">From this candidate word set, we constructed 80 word triplets in which the nouns were transformed from singulars to plurals (e.g., from <em>stain</em> to <em>stains</em> ). </span><span class="sentence">Half of the triplets consisted of categorically related nouns, which we refer to as <em>aligned-categorical</em>, or AC, <em>triplets</em> (e.g., <em>oaks</em>, <em>elms</em>, and <em>maples</em>).  The other half consisted of unrelated nouns, which we refer to as <em>misaligned-unrelated</em>, or MU, <em>triplets</em> (e.g., <em>marbles</em>, <em>guards</em>, and <em>ponds</em>). </span><span class="sentence">The two sets of AC and MU triplets were matched for word frequency, familiarity, concreteness, and syllable count. </span><span class="sentence">The AC and MU word triplets appear, respectively, in Table 1 and Table 2.</span><br/><br/> <img class="rs_skip" src="/paper_data/img/xlm-34-2-343-tbl1a.gif" alt="xlm-34-2-343-tbl1a.gif"/><em>Aligned Categorically Related Word Triplets</em><br/><br/> <img class="rs_skip" src="/paper_data/img/xlm-34-2-343-tbl2a.gif" alt="xlm-34-2-343-tbl2a.gif"/><em>Misaligned Unrelated Word Triplets</em></p><p class="body-paragraph" data-auto="body_paragraph"><span class="sentence">In each word triplet, two words were designated to serve as the priming word pair and the third as the target. </span><span class="sentence">In order to balance the number of “yes” and “no” responses, half of the target words matched one of the priming words (correct response is “yes”) and the other half did not (correct response is “no”). </span><span class="sentence">Specifically, of the 40 AC triplets, 20 had a matching target (e.g., <em>horses</em>, <em>donkeys</em>, and <em>horses</em>) and 20 a nonmatching AC target (e.g., <em>ants</em>, <em>beetles</em>, and <em>spiders</em>). </span><span class="sentence">Similarly, of the 40 MU triplets, 20 had a matching target (e.g., <em>gowns</em>, <em>bridges</em>, and <em>bridges</em>) and 20 a nonmatching MU target (e.g., <em>napkins</em>, <em>cats</em>, and <em>authors</em>). </span><span class="sentence">Half of the matching targets matched the left, and the other half matched the right words of the priming pairs.</span></p><p class="body-paragraph" data-auto="body_paragraph"><span class="sentence">Relatedness ratings for all the priming word pairs used in Experiment 1 were obtained from a subset of participants (<em>n</em> = 33) after they had completed all the experimental trials of the word-digit matching task. </span><span class="sentence">These participants rated the extent to which the priming word pairs were related to each other on a 7-point Likert scale, with 1 indicating that the words were completely unrelated and 7 indicating that the words were highly related. </span><span class="sentence">Each participant provided ratings to 60 word pairs that were selected at random from the 80 priming pairs used in the experiment. </span><span class="sentence">AC pairs (<em>M</em> = 5.97, <em>SD</em> = 0.73) received significantly higher relatedness ratings than the MU pairs (<em>M</em> =1.42, <em>SD</em> = 0.42), <em>t</em>(32) = 37.60, <em>p</em> &lt; .001.</span></p><p class="body-paragraph" data-auto="body_paragraph"><em>Digit triplets</em></p><p class="body-paragraph" data-auto="body_paragraph"><span class="sentence">Forty-eight digit triplets were adapted from the “standard” stimuli cues used by Lefevre and Kulak (1994). </span><span class="sentence">Each digit triplet consisted of a pair of cue digits and a digit target. </span><span class="sentence">The cue digits (<em>m</em> + <em>n</em>) were of the form <em>m</em> &gt; 1, <em>n</em> &gt; 1, and <em>m</em> ≠ <em>n</em> (e.g., 3 + 2). </span><span class="sentence">Half of the digit targets matched one of the cue digits and half did not. </span><span class="sentence">Half of the matching targets matched the left and half matched the right cue digit. </span><span class="sentence">The complete set of digit stimuli used in Experiment 1 appears in Table 3.</span><br/><br/> <img class="rs_skip" src="/paper_data/img/xlm-34-2-343-tbl3a.gif" alt="xlm-34-2-343-tbl3a.gif"/><em>Digit Triplets</em></p><p class="body-paragraph" data-auto="body_paragraph"><span class="sentence">Half of the triplets had matching targets (correct response “yes”), and the other half had targets that did not match either one of the cue digits (correct response “no”). </span><span class="sentence">Only triplets with nonmatching digit targets are relevant for assessing the sum effect (24 triplets total). </span><span class="sentence">The cue digits of these triplets appear in the leftmost column of Table 3. </span><span class="sentence">The target digits ranged from 5 to 9 and, depending on the cue digits, served as either sum or neutral targets. </span><span class="sentence">Twelve triplets consisted of the cue digits and their sum target (e.g., 5, for the cue digits 3 + 2), and 12 consisted of the cue digits and a neutral target (e.g., 7).</span></p><p class="body-paragraph" data-auto="body_paragraph"><span class="sentence">In the other 24 triplets, the targets matched one of the cue digits (correct answer is “yes”). </span><span class="sentence">Half of the matching target triplets served as <em>cue</em> controls. </span><span class="sentence">In these 12 triplets, the cue digits were the same as those used in the nonmatching sum and neutral triplets (e.g., 3 + 2; see the leftmost column of Table 3) and were in the 2–6 digit range. </span><span class="sentence">The other half of the matching target triplets served as <em>target</em> controls. </span><span class="sentence">These 12 triplets were constructed to ensure that the matching targets (“yes” response) covered the same digit range (5–9) as the nonmatching targets (“no” response). </span><span class="sentence">Accordingly, the cue digits in the target control triplets (e.g., 8 + 4; see the rightmost column of Table 3) had sums larger than 10.</span></p><p class="body-paragraph" data-auto="body_paragraph"><em>Apparatus</em></p><p class="body-paragraph" data-auto="body_paragraph"><span class="sentence">Written instructions and the experimental trials were presented on 17-in. (43-cm) monitors controlled by PC computers. </span><span class="sentence">Responses to digit and word targets (yes and no) and response latencies were recorded with the use of Inquisit software Version 1.31 software (Inquisit, 2000), which is accurate to 0.08 ms. All stimuli presentation times were multiples of the screen refresh rate of 16.7 ms.</span></p><p class="body-paragraph" data-auto="body_paragraph"><strong>Procedure</strong></p><p class="body-paragraph" data-auto="body_paragraph"><span class="sentence">In order to ensure that people attend to both the priming words and the cue digits, on each experimental trial, participants had to make two matching decisions (“Did you see X? yes/no”): one to a word target (e.g., X = <em>ants</em>; X = <em>spiders</em>) and one to a digit target (e.g., X = <em>3</em>; X = <em>9</em>). </span><span class="sentence">To discourage dual-tasking strategies, on half of the trials, the digit target appeared first and was followed by the word target, and on the other half, the word target appeared first. </span><span class="sentence">Although participants responded to both word and digit targets, only trials in which the digit targets appeared first (i.e., immediately after the cue digits) preserved the structure of the original number-matching task (LeFevre et al., 1988). </span><span class="sentence">These trials provide the cleanest test of whether semantically aligned or misaligned word pairs would prime the sum effect. </span><span class="sentence">Hence, we only report the results pertaining to the latencies and error rates of the relevant digit targets in the digit-first condition, comparing the trials in which these digits were primed by the semantically aligned and the semantically misaligned word pairs.</span></p><p class="body-paragraph" data-auto="body_paragraph"><span class="sentence">Participants were tested in small groups of 4–13. </span><span class="sentence">They received verbal and written instructions explaining that they would see pairs of words and digits and, subsequently, see word and digit probes. </span><span class="sentence">Their task was to respond “yes” if the probe was one of the items that were just presented and “no” if it was not. </span><span class="sentence">Participants were instructed to place the index finger of their preferred hand on the <em>2</em> key on the numerical keypad of the keyboard. </span><span class="sentence">They had to indicate their “yes” and “no” responses by pressing the <em>1</em> and <em>3</em> (or <em>3</em> and <em>1</em>) keys, respectively, and then return their index finger to the centered <em>2</em> key. </span><span class="sentence">They were instructed to respond as quickly as possible without sacrificing accuracy. </span><span class="sentence">Participants were also told that they would be taking a memory recognition test at the end of the word-digit matching task to test their memory for the words seen during the experiment. </span><span class="sentence">This instruction was intended to encourage semantic processing of the words (Schreuder, Giovanni, &amp; Glazenborg, 1984), but we did not administer the memory test.</span></p><p class="body-paragraph" data-auto="body_paragraph"><span class="sentence">Figure 1 depicts the sequence of events on each experimental trial. </span><span class="sentence">Each trial began with an asterisk prompt in the middle of the screen. </span><span class="sentence">The prompt remained on the screen until the participant pressed the <em>2</em> key on the numeric keypad of the keyboard. </span><span class="sentence">At this keypress, a randomly selected pair of priming words appeared on the screen. </span><span class="sentence">The words were presented in line-drawn boxes, one box on the left and the other on the right side of the screen's center. </span><span class="sentence">After 480 ms of this words display, a pair of randomly selected cue digits appeared in the boxes above the words, and a <em>plus</em> sign appeared between the two boxes. </span><span class="sentence">The priming words and the cue digits remained on the screen for an additional 135 ms, at which point the stimuli were erased and masked for 16.7 ms. The computer then randomly selected either a digit or a word target and displayed it at the center of the screen. </span><span class="sentence">This target remained on the screen until the participant responded, or for a maximum of 2 s.</span><br/><br/> <img class="rs_skip" src="/paper_data/img/xlm-34-2-343-fig1a.gif" alt="xlm-34-2-343-fig1a.gif"/><em>Figure 1. An illustration of the experimental procedure. Asterisk represents the prompt used at the outset of each trial.</em></p><p class="body-paragraph" data-auto="body_paragraph"><span class="sentence">Participants pressed the designated <em>yes</em> or <em>no</em> keys to indicate whether the target digit or word was presented in that trial. </span><span class="sentence">They received written feedback, either “RIGHT” in green letters or “WRONG” in red letters, which remained on the screen for 500 ms. Lack of response within 2 s also received the feedback “WRONG.” </span><span class="sentence">After the feedback to the first target disappeared, the second target was presented at the center of the screen. </span><span class="sentence">In the digit-first condition, the second target was a word; in the word-first condition, the second target was a digit. </span><span class="sentence">Participants responded to the second target in the same manner they responded to the first. </span><span class="sentence">Consecutive trials were separated by a forced 2-s delay.</span></p><p class="body-paragraph" data-auto="body_paragraph"><span class="sentence">Each participant received a unique randomized sequence of 160 trials that had a balanced 4 (word triplet) × 4 (digit triplet) ×2 (target order) factorial design, with five trials in each experimental cell. </span><span class="sentence">The four word-triplet conditions were AC prime/matching target, AC prime/nonmatching target, MU prime/matching target, and MU prime/nonmatching target. </span><span class="sentence">The four digit-triplet conditions were sum, neutral, cue control, and target control. </span><span class="sentence">The two target-order conditions were digit first and word first. </span><span class="sentence">The word triplets were selected at random without replacement until all 80 triplets were exhausted and then sampled again for a total of 160 trials. </span><span class="sentence">The digit triplets were selected at random without replacement until all 48 triplets were exhausted, and this sampling was repeated for a total of 160 trials. </span><span class="sentence">The constraints on the random draw of the word and digit combinations were such that there were no more than two consecutive identical trial types and no more than four identical consecutive correct responses (e.g., “no”).</span></p><p class="body-paragraph" data-auto="body_paragraph"><span class="sentence">Participants received 20 practice trials that were similar to the experimental trials. </span><span class="sentence">Although we wanted participants to get familiar with the matching task, we did not want them to think about the relation between the words and the digits. </span><span class="sentence">Accordingly, all the word triplets in the practice trials were unrelated (e.g., <em>notes</em>, <em>cords</em>, and <em>tubes</em>). </span><span class="sentence">These word triplets were not used in the subsequent experimental trials. </span><span class="sentence">The practice trials were followed by a brief clarification break during which the experimenter verified that the participants understood the task. </span><span class="sentence">The participants then proceeded to perform the 160 experimental trials. </span><span class="sentence">They took approximately 12 min to complete this task.</span></p>
