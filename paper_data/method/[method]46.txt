<h3>Study 4</h3><br/><p class="body-paragraph" data-auto="body_paragraph"><button class="sentence">In Study 4, we used a between-subjects design to test the effects of target gender and ethnicity on pride recognition.</button></p> <h4>Method</h4><p class="body-paragraph" data-auto="body_paragraph"><strong>Participants</strong></p><p class="body-paragraph" data-auto="body_paragraph"><button class="sentence">Participants were 211 undergraduate students (60% women) from the University of California, Davis, who participated in exchange for course credit. </button><button class="sentence">Participants self-identified their race as Asian (47%), Caucasian (31%), Latino (9%), African American (2%), and other or mixed (11%).</button></p><p class="body-paragraph" data-auto="body_paragraph"><strong>Stimuli</strong></p><p class="body-paragraph" data-auto="body_paragraph"><button class="sentence">The set of drawn figure targets that was developed for Study 3 was used.</button></p><p class="body-paragraph" data-auto="body_paragraph"><strong>Procedure</strong></p><p class="body-paragraph" data-auto="body_paragraph"><button class="sentence">Participants (<em>N</em> = 211) were randomly assigned to one of six ethnic–gender conditions. </button><button class="sentence">Participants in each condition viewed only a single target (African man, African woman, Asian man, Asian woman, Caucasian man, or Caucasian woman) showing all eight emotion expressions. </button><button class="sentence">Participants viewed each of the eight images, projected onto a large 4-ft × 6-ft (1.2-m × 1.8-m) screen, for 30 s. The order of the images was randomized. </button><button class="sentence">As in Study 3, participants were asked, for each image, to “choose the emotion that best matches the emotion expressed by the person in the slide” from the following list of options: <em>anger</em>, <em>contempt</em>, <em>disgust</em>, <em>fear</em>, <em>happiness</em>, <em>pride</em>, <em>sadness</em>, <em>surprise</em>, and <em>none of these are correct</em>. </button><button class="sentence">Participants were also again asked to rate the intensity of the emotion expressed on a 7-point scale ranging from 0 (<em>not at all intense</em>) to 6 (<em>extremely intense</em>).</button></p>
