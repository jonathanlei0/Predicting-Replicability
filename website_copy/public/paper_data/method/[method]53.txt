<div class="sectionInfo"><h2 class="sectionHeading">METHOD</h2></div><div class="head-b"><h3>Participants</h3></div><p><span class="sentence">Two hundred eighty heterosexual undergraduate men (63.6%) and women (36.4%) participated in exchange for course credit. </span><span class="sentence">The sample was predominantly White-Caucasian (85.4%; 3.6% African American, 5.0% Asian or Asian American, 2.5% Hispanic, 3.6% other), and the average age was 19.6 years ( <i>SD</i> = 1.72).</span></p><div class="head-b"><h3>Identification Task</h3></div><p><span class="sentence">Seated in a private computer room, participants categorized each of a series of photo images of women into one of four categories: friendly, sexually interested, sad, or rejecting. </span><span class="sentence">Each participant was randomly assigned to view the images for 500 ms or 3,000 ms. </span><span class="sentence">The 500-ms presentation time was sufficiently short to make it challenging to decode all relevant information thoroughly; the 3,000-ms presentation time provided ample opportunity for thorough processing. </span><span class="sentence">Participants viewed the images in four blocks of 70 randomly ordered images, with a 30-s pause separating successive blocks.</span></p><div class="head-b"><h3>Stimulus Set</h3></div><p><span class="sentence">Extensive pilot testing produced a set of 280 full-body images of clothed women displaying one of the four dating-relevant emotions. </span><span class="sentence">Initially, a sample of 497 undergraduate men provided normative ratings of 1,127 images of undergraduate women who displayed a variety of emotions and wore different clothing styles. </span><span class="sentence">The men used 7-point scales to rate how friendly, sexually interested, sad, rejecting, attractive, and provocatively dressed each woman was. </span><span class="sentence">Those images rated above the median on the scales of interest were retained for consideration. </span><span class="sentence">From this group, we selected images such that approximately half of the retained images were rated above the midpoint for provocative dress. </span><span class="sentence">Note that the women in the photos were instructed to select clothing from their own wardrobes; therefore, the variance along the clothing-style continuum is representative of the range of clothing styles that a college student would observe among his or her peers.</span></p><p><span class="sentence">As the final step in generating the photo set, we asked a new sample of undergraduates (80 men and 80 women) to categorize the women in the photographs as friendly, sexually interested, sad, or rejecting. </span><span class="sentence">A photograph was retained if the majority of men and the majority of women categorized the picture into the same affect group. </span><span class="sentence">We used a majority criterion, rather than a strong-consensus criterion, to ensure some variability within each category, as variability is required to model the theoretically relevant perception parameters. </span><span class="sentence">Finally, in selecting the photographs to be used in the experiment, we examined attractiveness ratings from the original normative sample and ensured that attractiveness was held constant across the four target types. </span><span class="sentence">The final set of 280 photographs consisted of 70 images in each of the four affect categories.</span></p><div class="head-b"><h3>Analysis</h3></div><p><span
